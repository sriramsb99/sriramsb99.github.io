Sriram Bharadwaj

PhD Student in Theoretical Physics

I am a PhD Candidate in Theoretical Physics at UCLA. My research sits at the intersection of Hamiltonian lattice gauge theory and quantum computation. I am broadly interested in reformulating lattice gauge theories in ways that make them more amenable to quantum simulation, and in developing quantum algorithms and simulation methods for classically hard problems. My recent work has included VQE simulations for lattice gauge theory, QAOA-inspired methods for continuous optimization, and adiabatic state preparation. Earlier in my PhD, I worked on theoretical aspects of quantum field theory and string theory. I am fortunate to be co-supervised by Eric D’Hoker in Physics and Leo Zhou in Electrical and Computer Engineering.

Research Interests

  • Lattice gauge theory and quantum computation. Developing Hamiltonian formulations of lattice gauge theories for efficient quantum simulation in sign-problem-affected regimes, including work on (2+1)D QED.
  • Quantum state learning. Applying machine learning methods to quantum many-body systems, while using tools from physics and information theory to analyze the representation power of neural-network ansätze.
  • Quantum algorithms. Developing efficient quantum algorithms for optimization problems and for the simulation of many-body systems on quantum hardware, including ongoing work on CV-QAOA.
  • Quantum simulations of many-body systems. Studying many-body physics that is inaccessible to classical computation through quantum simulation methods.
  • State preparation. Developing efficient state-preparation algorithms for many-body quantum simulations, including lattice gauge theories and condensed matter systems, with ongoing work on adiabatic state preparation for unbounded Hamiltonians.

Publications

Information-Theoretic Scaling Laws of Neural Quantum States

With Y. Lu, D. Rathore, and D. Luo — March 2026 — arXiv, Phys. Rev. Lett. to appear

  • Systematic study of the representation power of Transformers and RNNs for quantum state learning.
  • Proves advantages of neural networks over tensor networks for states with (sub-)volume-law dephased mutual information.
  • Derives information-theoretic scaling laws for a broad class of many-body systems.
  • Examples include stabilizer states, Rokhsar–Kivelson states, the toric code, and the finite-temperature transverse-field Ising model.

Hamiltonian Formulation of Lattice QED3 with One and Two Flavors of Wilson Fermions: Topological Structure and Response

With D. Luo, L. Funcke, et al. — March 2026 — arXiv, Phys. Rev. D to appear

  • Extends earlier analysis to include dynamical U(1) gauge fields.
  • Investigates classically non-simulable regimes through a Hamiltonian formulation.
  • Analyzes the topological phase diagram at finite density with one and two fermion flavors, beyond the reach of standard classical simulations due to the sign problem.
  • Maps the topological phase diagram at finite gauge coupling.
  • Identifies order parameters for topological phase transitions relevant for near-term quantum simulations via VQE.

A Path to Quantum Simulations of Topological Phases: (2+1)D Quantum Electrodynamics with Wilson Fermions

With D. Luo, L. Funcke, et al. — April 2025 — arXiv, Phys. Rev. Lett. to appear

  • Shows that staggered-fermion approaches fail to capture certain novel topological phases.
  • Proposes an alternative Wilson-fermion formulation that faithfully captures the relevant topological structure.
  • Provides exact-diagonalization proof-of-concept results demonstrating feasibility for near-term quantum devices.

Selected Ongoing Research

CV-QAOA: Efficient Low-depth Continuous Quantum Optimization

With Di Luo and Leo Zhou

  • Develops a low-overhead quantum optimization algorithm for high-dimensional problems.
  • Inherits the flexibility and practical appeal of discrete QAOA.
  • Aims at super-polynomial speedups in non-convex optimization relative to the best known classical algorithms.
  • Provides black-box query-complexity separations between classical and quantum algorithms in provable instances.
  • Semi-finalist in the XPRIZE Quantum Applications competition (top 20 of 133 submissions).

Simulating Topological Phases on a Quantum Computer Using VQE

With Di Luo et al.

  • Uses VQE to simulate topological phases based on the Hamiltonian formulation introduced in arXiv:2504.21828.
  • Prepares the ground state of Wilson fermions coupled to U(1) gauge fields using a QAOA-inspired ansatz.
  • Develops rigorous estimates of the necessary gate complexity.
  • Aims to run the simulation on real quantum hardware to probe the predicted phase diagram.
  • Demonstrates the potential of quantum computers to simulate novel topological phases of matter.
